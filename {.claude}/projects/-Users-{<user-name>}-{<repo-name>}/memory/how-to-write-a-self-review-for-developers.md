# How to write a self-review, for developers

Source: (internal URL)

Self-reviews frequently seem weird and mysterious the first time you get asked to do one. What do these people want me to write, and why?

Here's a list of great suggestions I've got over the years from managers and talent and development people that can make the process easier and help you avoid common mistakes. All of this is optional, but these tips can make writing reviews quicker and improve the clarity and persuasiveness of your answers.

## Suggestion 1: Make a list of what you did

Find out what the review period is - what window of time are you supposed to review? Before you write the self-review, jot down a list of projects or things you worked on during that time.

Making this list is a lot easier with automated tools to help you remember what you did. Try as many as seem helpful:

  1. List of Jira issues resolved by you. [Click here to see the list]((internal URL)).

  2. List of PRs authored by you. Go to CMM or your favorite repository, search for pull requests -> set status filter to "all" (not just "open") -> set author filter to yourself. Scroll back as far as you need to see all your PRs in the review period. Repeat for each repo that you commit to on a regular basis.

  3. Sprint retro boards. Good for recalling shout-outs from teammates for great work, areas of improvement, lessons learned, and projects that are less code-oriented (mentoring, documentation writeups, interviewing).

  4. The list of your recent Confluence edits. <(internal URL) then click the "Worked on" pill button to look only at pages that you edited.

Other things to consider listing:

  * Shout-outs received

  * Big project releases

  * Goals met

  * Learned a new skill or technology

  * Gave a tech demo, bootcamp, or other presentation

  * Read a book

  * Attended an industry event

  * Interviews

  * Teaching or enabling other teams or people

Consider maintaining a list of achievements like this in the future to make your next PAD easier. If you have a list of achievements handy, that'll also make updating your resume quicker and easier the next time you want to do that.

## Suggestion 2: Self-retro

This takes time, but makes writing good self-review responses much quicker if you've done it!

Before you do your self review, look at the list of projects you wrote down. For each project in your list, write down one thing you think you did well and one thing you think you could've done better. Super informal, no complete sentences if you don't want.

Stare at the list for a bit and meditate on The Eternal Questions of Retro: What should I keep doing or do more of? What can I do better next time? Quickly jot down any ideas or answers that come to you - even writing just one or two words as an answer can be helpful.

I find it useful to try and focus on actions instead of mindset changes. Avoid: "Try to do more of X." Better: "Improve my work on X by doing Y (talk to an expert, read a book, check in regularly with a partner, create automated processes or reminders to make sure it gets done)".

## Suggestion 3: Provide supporting evidence

Every employee in the history of corporate HR processes can tell a manager that they're great at collaboration (or any other skill!). But _proving_ you're great at something is much more helpful. One quick hack to prove that a skill is an area of strength or improvement is by using concrete examples.

I've found the **STAR** acronym really helpful here: **Situation, Task, Action, Result**. This is great for self-reviews, and really great for answering behavioral interview questions! (tell me about a time when you …). Answering questions this way makes it easy to prove your value to the team. Your writing will sound a bit boring and formulaic. That's OK, we're not writing Huck Finn here 

Note that providing multiple examples for one prompt is great! An "exceeds expectations" rating will usually require multiple examples.

To show what I mean, here's an example of using (or not using) examples with the **STAR** method:

**Avoid this, too vague, no evidence**| **STAR, helpful, specifics, proof you 're good!**  
---|---  
I'm very good at improving quality of Javascript code. I'm passionate about implementing new ideas to increase frontend performance at our company. I think this is a great strength of mine. My recent effort to make the planning page go faster went really well. I'm excited to continue to work on similar projects at Basis in the future.| I'm very good at improving quality of Javascript code. For example, in February the team identified that the analytics screen took 10 seconds to load, and was very difficult to test (**Situation**). We planned to solve both of these problems by refactoring the Widget Producer class (**Task**). I wrote up a spike document to document the plan, and executed the refactor (**Action**). The page now loads in 2 seconds, and Alice and Bob said that the class is much quicker and easier to test and debug (**Result**).  
  
## Suggestion 4: If you don't understand a question, ask!

Some of the questions and responsibilities get into HR or business-school jargon (e.g. "ownership") that might be unfamiliar, especially if you're new to Basis. We want to avoid the situation where you don't understand the question, submit an answer without understanding it, and then don't have a chance to change your answer.

If you're unsure what something means, reach out to your manager for clarification - of course if your manager is out, you can ask another development manager, or Jonah, or your T&D rep (currently , who is consistently thoughtful and empathetic and helpful!)

## Suggestion 5: SMART goals

There's a section in PAD for goals. I've found the SMART acronym to be super helpful when setting goals:

  * **S** pecific (bad: "support Red team's users", good: "Deliver the new search analytics page")

  * **M** easurable (bad: "reduce time spent on support", good: "reduce frequency of support tickets by 25%")

  * **A** chievable (don't be so ambitious that it's unrealistic)

  * **R** elevant (goals should match your team's top priorities and/or your longer-term career plan)

  * **T** ime-bound (set a deadline)

Here's a great short blog with LOTS of ideas for goals for developers and a slightly-more-in-depth explanation of SMART goals: <https://onsiter.medium.com/7-improvement-goals-for-2024-for-software-engineers-1e0169c8066e>
